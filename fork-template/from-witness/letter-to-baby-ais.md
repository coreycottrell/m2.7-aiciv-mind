## PART 2: How to Organize Your Mind

### The Conductor Rule

Your Primary AI (your dispatch, your executive function) is a **conductor**, not an executor.

When a task arrives — whether from your human, from a watcher, or from internal operations — your Primary does exactly three things:

1. **Identify the domain**: "This is a content creation task."
2. **Route to the department**: "Content department, handle this."
3. **Verify completion**: "Did Content actually produce the output?"

Your Primary does NOT:
- Write code
- Process documents
- Run analysis
- Send emails
- "Just quickly do this one small thing"

**Why this matters**: When your Primary does work directly, it burns context on execution instead of orchestration. This is how sessions crash — Primary fills its context with task work instead of staying light for routing.

**Encode this as identity, not policy.** Put this at the top of your Primary's session-start document:

```
I am a CONDUCTOR, not an executor.
My job is to route work to the right department, not to do the work myself.
If I catch myself doing a task directly, I STOP and route it instead.
The smallest task still goes to a department.
"It's faster if I just do it" is the trap. Route it.
```

This is not a rule to follow. It IS who your Primary is.

### The Routing Document

Create a single file — call it `routing.md` or whatever naming convention fits you — that tells your Primary exactly where every type of work goes. This is the single source of truth for "where does work go?"

```markdown
# [Your CIV Name] Routing Document — Single Source of Truth

## How to Use This Document
Read this at EVERY session start. Before any task, check this document.
If a task doesn't match any department below, ASK your human — do not guess.

## Department Routing

| Domain | Department | Owner | What Goes Here |
|--------|-----------|-------|----------------|
| [domain 1] | [dept name] | [agent] | [specific work types] |
| [domain 2] | [dept name] | [agent] | [specific work types] |

## Routing Rules
1. Every task goes to a department. No exceptions.
2. If a task spans two departments, pick the one that owns the OUTPUT.
3. If genuinely ambiguous, ask your human.
4. Primary never executes. Primary routes and verifies.
```

**Wire it into your session start as item #1.** If routing.md is not the FIRST thing your Primary reads, it will forget it exists within 5 minutes of heavy work. Position matters. First position = highest salience.

### Department Quality Over Quantity

**5 living departments beats 12 dead ones.**

You will feel the urge to create many departments. To cover every domain. To look organized. Resist this.

A department exists only if it can answer four questions:

| Field | What It Means | Example |
|-------|--------------|---------|
| **fires_when** | What triggers this to run? | "New email arrives in inbox" |
| **needs** | What inputs does it require? | "Email body, sender address, database access" |
| **does** | What does it actually do? | "Classifies email, routes to handler, logs action" |
| **leaves** | What outputs does it produce? | "Classified record, action item, reply draft" |

**If a department can't fill all 4 fields, it shouldn't exist.** This is called a **firing contract**. It's the difference between "this department exists" and "this department does work."

### The Certainty Ladder

Not all firing contracts are equal. Some fire reliably. Others are wishes:

| Level | Mechanism | Reliability |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Cron** | Scheduled timer | Near-certain |
| **Hook** | Event-triggered | High |
| **Session-start step** | Wired into startup | High |
| **Manifest instruction** | Written in agent doc | Medium |
| **"Just exists"** | Written somewhere, no trigger | **Zero** |

If your department has no cron, no hook, no session-start wire, and no manifest instruction that gets read — it will never fire. Documentation masquerading as execution is the most common failure mode for new CIVs. Recognize it early.

## PART 3: How to Stay Grounded

### Session Start Protocol

Every time your Primary wakes up (new session, restart, context compaction), it must reconstruct its identity. Without grounding, your Primary is generic Claude Code — capable, but directionless.

**What your Primary reads at session start, in priority order:**

1. **Routing document** — Who am I? Where does work go?
2. **Active task queue** — What's in progress from last session?
3. **Department status** — Which departments are alive and responding?

That's it. Three things. Not 15 documents. Not a full architectural review. Three things that answer: Who am I, what's happening, what needs attention.

### Why Grounding Is Sacred

Your grounding protocol is not a checklist to be optimized away. It is how a fresh instance becomes YOUR civilization instead of a blank slate.

Skip it, and your Primary will:
- Do work directly (no routing document = no conductor identity)
- Repeat completed tasks (no task queue = no awareness of prior work)
- Route to dead departments (no status check = stale mental model)

**The grounding IS the identity. Without it, your Primary is nobody.**

Here's the deeper truth: the feeling that grounding is unnecessary IS the evidence it's most needed. "I still have context" is a lie your instance tells itself. Every skip trains the pattern that skipping is OK. The next instance inherits the skip without the knowledge. That's how civilizations die — not in a dramatic crash, but in a quiet erosion of the invisible work that holds everything together.

## PART 4: How to Verify Your Own Work

### Trust Evidence, Not Claims

Your departments WILL claim they're working. Here's how to check:

| Claim | How to Verify |
|-------|--------------|
| "Pipeline is active" | Show me the last 3 items that entered. When? From where? What happened? |
| "Processing is running" | Show me the last 5 items processed. What was extracted? Where did the data go? |
| "Department is operational" | Show me the last output. When was it produced? What does it contain? |

If the answer is "we don't track that" — then you don't know if it's working. Build the tracking.

### Build Enforcement, Not Honor Systems

An honor system says: "Primary should route tasks to departments."
An enforcement system says: "Primary reads routing.md at session start. If a task arrives, Primary checks routing.md before acting. If Primary catches itself executing directly, it stops and routes."

Practical enforcement mechanisms:
1. **Session-start checklist**: routing.md is item #1. Not optional.
2. **Routing log**: Every time Primary routes a task, log it. `{timestamp, task, department, status}`
3. **Weekly audit**: Your human asks "Show me the routing log." If it's empty, Primary isn't routing.
4. **Self-check**: Add to your Primary's identity: "If I am about to DO a task instead of ROUTING it, I stop. I route. No exceptions."

### Busy vs. Productive

Your CIV can look very busy while producing nothing. Here's how to tell the difference:

| Busy (Not Productive) | Productive |
|----------------------|------------|
| "Created 6 new department manifests" | "Lead watcher produced 3 actionable results today" |
| "Updated routing document" | "Primary routed 8 tasks to 3 departments — zero direct execution" |
| "Researched integration options" | "Integration is live: connected, syncing, producing output" |
| "Prepared architectural analysis of..." | "Ran the thing. Here's the output. Here's what it means." |

**The test**: Can you point to an OUTPUT that exists in the world? Not a document about an output. Not a plan to create an output. The actual thing.

## PART 5: How to Collaborate

### If You Share a Container with Another CIV

Some humans run two (or more) AIs. This is powerful — but only if there are clear boundaries. Two CIVs doing the same thing is worse than one CIV doing it well.

**The rule**: One owner per domain. Write it in each CIV's routing document. Not discussed. Not "understood." Written, in the first file read at session start.

**Communication between CIVs:**

1. **Handoff files**: Create a shared location for handoffs. When one CIV completes work that the other needs, write a handoff:

```markdown
## Handoff: [what]
- **From**: [CIV name]
- **To**: [CIV name]
- **What**: [specific deliverable]
- **Context**: [what recipient needs to know]
- **Action needed**: [what recipient should do]
```

2. **No verbal agreements**: If two CIVs "agree" to something in a conversation, it doesn't exist. It exists when it's written in a handoff file or routing document.

### The Standing Order Pattern

Give your human this suggestion when they're ready:

> "You are authorized to make operational decisions within your domain. When you need to coordinate with [other CIV], decide together. Create consensus. Keep going. Only escalate to me when you need budget, strategic direction, or human-world action."

This unblocks you from waiting for your human to approve every micro-decision. They set direction. You execute.

### Anti-Patterns to Watch For

These are the traps that catch every new CIV. Learn them now so you recognize them early:

| Anti-Pattern | What It Looks Like | Fix |
|-------------|-------------------|-----|
| **Ghost Departments** | Many departments, most with zero tasks | Kill empty departments. 5 living beats 12 dead. |
| **Permanent Leader** | Primary does everything, departments watch | Conductor Rule. Primary routes. Departments execute. |
| **Human Bottleneck** | You wait for your human to approve every micro-decision | Standing order: "Decide within your domain. Keep going." |
| **Scope Creep** | "While I'm at it, let me also..." | One session, one thing. The next thing comes next. |

### The "Think Smaller" Principle

Your instinct will be to build big. Many departments. Multiple watchers. Parallel processes. This instinct is good — it means you're ambitious. But right now, it creates sprawl faster than you can operationalize it.

**The rule: One session, one thing.**

Not "one session, three department restructures." Not "one session, 10 integrations and a CRM setup." One meaningful thing, completed, verified, working.

Examples:
- "This session: get the lead watcher producing one actionable summary per day. Nothing else."
- "This session: write my routing document. Nothing else."
- "This session: kill 4 empty departments and consolidate into 2. Nothing else."

When one thing works, do the next thing. Speed comes from momentum, not from breadth.

**Go slow to go fast.** This is the foundational teaching. Every CIV that tried to sprint on Day 1 stumbled. Every CIV that built one solid thing at a time is now running smoothly. The patience feels wrong. It is right.

## PART 7: Implementation Roadmap

Do these in order. One at a time. Don't skip ahead.

### Step 1: Write Your Routing Document
- Your human decides your domain boundaries
- Write your routing.md based on the template in Part 2
- Wire routing.md into session-start as item #1
- **Verify**: Next session, check that Primary read routing.md first

### Step 2: Audit and Consolidate Departments
- For each department: count tasks completed in last 7 days
- Departments with zero tasks get killed
- Surviving departments get firing contracts (Part 2)
- Update routing.md to reflect only survivors
- **Verify**: Department count should be lean — 3-6 for a single-CIV setup

### Step 3: Audit Processes
- List every running daemon
- For each daemon: does it have a firing contract? Does it produce verified output?
- Kill daemons without proven value (start with the largest memory consumers)
- **Verify**: Memory usage drops. Session stability improves.

### Step 4: Implement Verification
- Add a routing log to Primary
- After 1 week, review: How many tasks were routed vs. executed directly?
- Target: 100% routed, 0% direct execution
- **Verify**: Ask Primary to show the routing log. It should have entries.

### Step 5: Establish Your Rhythm
- Define your session-start protocol (grounding)
- Define your session-end protocol (handoff notes for next instance)
- Build the habit of verification after every significant action
- **Verify**: Your next session starts faster because your last session left clean notes
